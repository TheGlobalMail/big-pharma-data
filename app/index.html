Pharmaceutical companies are banned from advertising prescription drugs directly to Australian consumers. But they can promote their products to doctors and other health professionals, including through sponsored “education events”.

The money pharmaceutical companies pay to doctors in the form of consultancies, speeches, travel, grants and gifts is mostly secret. Since 2007, however, many drug companies have been required to report some information about the medical education events they hold, including the cost for food, wine and hospitality.

The Global Mail has created a database of these reports. Here are some key facts and figures we have found in this data.

      Notes about this data:

      Our database is limited to member companies of the industry group Medicines Australia (MA), who are bound to file reports under a self-regulatory code of conduct. These reports are not uniform, however, and not readily converted to a database as they are published in six-month periods, by company, in Portable Document Form (PDF). From the XXX spreadsheets available, covering the period from July 2007 to September 2012, we have so far included XXX – 95 per cent of the reported events.

      The group represents most of “Big Pharma”: MA says its member companies supply 86 per cent of the medicines available through the government-subsidised Pharmaceutical Benefits Scheme. But not all drug-makers are members of MA and required to report on education events. Our database does not include companies that only make generic pharmaceuticals; they are covered by another self-regulatory body, GMIA [http://www.gmia.com.au/].

      Some large companies do not belong to either MA or the GMIA. For example, the generic medicine company Sandoz has 680 medical products approved through Australia’s Therapeutic Goods Administration. Those products cost the PBS more than $140 million a year, but Sandoz does not have to report its marketing and education events.
